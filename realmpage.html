  • Solo Project   |   Personal Case Study   |   Three weeks (Summer 2017)

    • Technologies are drastically evolving. With enhanced file encryption and privacy protection, instant upload and download speed, and the rolling out of 5G, the foundations are set to revolutionize cloud storage services. These services will likely experience an explosive growth in the near future. User behaviors will be dramatically changed. Cloud storage will become the new local drive.

    • View Prototype

  • Business Problem

    Design a product that enables users to create, upload, organize, and share content, save content found online, and collaborate with other users to create content together.

  • Proposed Solution

    Conduct user research to establish a well-defined position from which to cut into the market. Develop an MVP that includes client-requested features that are backed up by research. Create a brand that differs from the competitors in the market. Deliver simple, delightful user experience and design for the target audience.

  • Key Findings

    1. Develop for casual to professional users.
    2. Target developing countries.
    3. Find a medium to actively and positively expose brand image to users.

  • Naming Brainstorm

    Brainstorming the name of the brand begins with creatively thinking about the core values of a cloud storage application. “Realm” was chosen after weighing the values of friendliness, conciseness, and attractiveness.

  • Logo Development

    To visualize the logo, I underwent a series of brainstorming sessions from many directions, letting my train of thought flow by itself. Finally, I was inspired by the idea of a dieline as a unique way of representing the idea of space, storage, and Realm. By following the formal logic of dielines, the following iterations took their course, and I finally settled on the one currently used for Realm. I manipulated the use of negative space by turning it into positive space and vice versa before deciding on the final iterations.

  • Preference Testing

    I was rather astonished to hear the perspectives of other people, since they saw things I did not notice myself. The three shapes used in the logo were final deductions based on the formal logic of dielines, but one participant realized the simple shapes of one logo consist of “two parallel laptops,” further enhancing the notion of collaboration. A delightful accident came from these spontaneous iterations. That was the moment I decided, “This is the logo I am using!”

  • Conclusion

  • Problems

    Lack of incentive for users to switch cloud storage providers

    The MVP lacks the incentive for users to switch over to a new cloud storage provider. Dropbox’s huge dropout rate (59.1%) comes from its dominance of usage and integration into other applications of Google Drive. How would Realm compete against Google Drive?

    Lack of diversification

    Realm also lacks diversification. In order to avoid direct competition with main competitors such as Google Drive, iCloud, or OneDrive, the product should diversify into tailoring a specific area. But this is not what the product has done. It is a general cloud storage provider like any other major cloud storage provider.

    Research biases

    In the research phase, most of the participants were university students with no to low salaries who fall mostly under the 20-30 age group. Frustrations that occur with them, such has expensive subscription, may not be a case for older users. This can be viewed as a research bias, but it also raises the question of how Realm can better serve this particular niche. There was no specific solution to research bias.

    Frustrations that cannot be solved by design

    Issues outside the scope of design that concern users the most are: privacy and security (backend), pricing (marketing), and storage space (backend and marketing). I could not improve these issues. However, the remaining issues were not immediate frustrations to the users.

    User survey was too general

    The survey remained on the superficial level of looking into users’ needs. The survey did not offer a strong, compelling justification of the necessity of creating a new product. In order to dig further into users’ potential needs, subsequent surveys are needed. The surveys need to be specific and impactful.

    Business problem was too ambiguous

    Lastly, the business problem was not clear or unique. It involved delivering a product upon request rather than providing a specific solution to a problem.

  • Improvements

    With more time, I would make the following modifications to my process:
    1. Research a clear business problem that needs to be tackled and provide a down-to-earth solution, instead of taking a client-agency stimulation.
    2. Produce more than one round of surveys to really understand users’ frustrations and pain points.
    3. Find problems that can be solved through a design method.
    4. Look for better ways to address research biases.
    5. Create a compelling product that the user really needs based on a more original idea.

  • What I've Learned

    Avoiding scope creep

    Avoiding scope creep was very challenging. It is easy to get caught up with details that might not matter to the bigger picture of the product’s design. Keeping the MVP in mind was very important.

    Acquiring and leveraging all the tools

    This was my first time utilizing all the tools needed to undertake a project that encompasses all the phases I went through. The whole process was exhilarating, and I had lots of fun. I became more competent at using the tools. I was slow at designing, due to unfamiliarity. If they are not critical to the process, some tools do not have to be involved in the future.

    Power of user feedback

    The power of user feedback is amazing, as well as interacting with potential users to figure out how they think.
